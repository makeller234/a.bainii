Since Google wasn't going to give me an easy time with this one, it was time to use some critical thinking.

First thought: Food. Animals can't survive without food, so maybe the type of food A. bainii ate survived the extinction as well. This source says that in the upper latitudes gymnosperms and conifer forests survived and that ferns survived in coastal regions. Unfortunately, A. bainii didn't live in theses areas. This source says that plants are relatively immune to mass extinction, but plant abundances and distributions can change.

Second Thought: Maybe it has to do with the type of animal it is? Aulacephalodon are Synapsids, or proto-mammals, and A.bainii is medium-sized compared to other dicynodonts. Large herbivores suffered the biggest losses. Which makes sense, as the plant abundance and distributions are changing, large herbivores need more food, so it would have been a lot harder for them to find adequate food. So A. bainii would have had a better chance at survival being medium-sized.
